Quick & Easy Sourdough Recipe

I love home baked bread, and over the last few years my partner and I have especially come to appreciate home-baked sourdough. There are a lot of really great recipes online and in books that I've used - this is not that. Here I'm just demonstrating some basic HTML skills. So with that in mind, here is a from-memory basic sourdough recipe. Enjoy!

Ingredients

Leaven

  • 5g ripe sourdough starter
  • 100g water
  • 100g flour

Dough

  • Leaven minus 5 grams removed as starter
  • 700g water
  • 1000g flour plus extra for shaping
  • 20g salt
  • 50g water

Shaping

  • Extra flour for shaping
  • Rice flour
  • Masa or cornmeal

Materials

  • Scale
  • Large glass or plastic bowl
  • Two bannetons or proofing containers
  • Sharp knife, scissors, or lame
  • Two dutch ovens
  • Cooling rack

Recipe

Mix the Leaven

  1. Take a bit of ripe sourdough starter and mix it with equal parts flour and water. We only keep a very small amount of sourdough starter, so we throw all our starter in here.
  2. If you put all your starter into the leaven mix, do not forget to remove some starter from the leaven prior to mixing the dough.
  3. Proceed to the next step when the leaven has roughly doubled, smells sweet, and will float in water. We generally wait overnight.
  4. If the leaven has become very sour, you can repeat this step again. I highly recommend making sourdough pancakes with any extra overly-mature leaven.

Make the dough

  1. Add the leaven and 700g of water to a large glass or plastic mixing bowl. Mix thoroughly - this makes mixing the flour much easier.
  2. Add 1000g of flour to the bowl. For beginners I would recommend 80/20 white/whole wheat or a bread flour, but you can play around with the type. Mix thoroughly (don't worry about kneading, just about getting all the flour wet) and let sit for an hour.
  3. Poke a bunch of holes in the top of the rested dough with your finger. Sprinkle 20-22g salt on top, then add a tiny bit more water (10-20g). Fold & squish the dough with your fingers until the salt & water are incorporated.
  4. Fold the dough approximately every half an hour for 2-3 hours then once an hour for another 2-3 hours. Total time & intervals between folding are dependent on a whole host of things, but at the end of the day you want lots of folding because it provides structure for your bread but not too much because at the end you can start to knock out air bubbles.
  5. When the dough is active and doubled in size, plop it on to a floured surface and divide it into two pieces. You can fold each into a ball and bed rest them 10-20 minutes, or you can shape them into loaves.
  6. Once shaped - and you'll have to google that, shaping is beyond this recipe - place each loaf in a banneton dusted with rice flour. Cover (I like to put them in an oversized plastic bag).
  7. Leave in bannetons for 1-3 hours at room temperature or refrigerated overnight.

Bake!

  1. 30-45 minutes before baking, place the dutch ovens in the oven, empty with lids askew, and set the oven for 500° F.
  2. Leave the oven heating up for at least 10 minutes after it get to temperature to make sure the dutch ovens are hot.
  3. Carefully remove the dutch ovens from the oven.
  4. Right before you place the dough in, put some masa or cornmeal in the bottom of the dutch oven to prevent the bottom of the bread from burning.
  5. Gently place the dough from the bannetons into the dutch ovens.
    • The side at the bottom of the bannetons should now be facing up.
    • If dough seams were placed at the bottom of the banneton and are now facing up, you may not need to score your bread - it will break naturally along the seams.
    • If the side facing you in the dutch oven is smooth, score the bread with a sharp knife, scissors, or lame.
  6. Place lids back on dutch ovens and put in oven. Immediately reduce temperature to 450° F.
  7. Bake for 20 minutes, then remove lids.
  8. Bake another 20-30 minutes, until the loaves are a nice dark brown.
  9. Remove from oven and place loaves on cooling rack.
  10. Listen to the bread do its joyous post-oven crackling.
  11. Cut immediately if you must, but the texture will be much better if you wait till it cools. Enjoy!
